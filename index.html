Motivation

Summary of user needs and motivating questions.

Expectation: There is a clear focus, and a developed explanation of the problem, and a reasonable response is proposed.

In our design, we focused on assessing the delay and unreliability of the buses. We focused on the routes that pass through Chester Square. The problem that we wanted to solve is highlighting the areas where the buses are most delayed/unreliable, so that people in a position to improve the buses would have this information. On our survey, a lot of people responded saying that the buses were unreliable and sometimes delayed, so we wanted to do something to highlight this problem. Therefore, our visualization will show users the reliability of each bus stop in Chester Square, and will also show users the reliability of the other stops on those bus routes which pass through Chester Square.

Visualization

Route Map of the Buses that run through or near Chester Square

What the coinciding visuals represent are a specified map of the Chester Square Neighborhood, and a local map of the city of boston. On the left we have a visual that has the stops placed at geographically correct coordinates within the chester square neighborhood. Each of the stops contains information about the stop name, and the route it is apart of.

On the right, we have a visual that displays the bus routes that most primarily affect the Chester Square Neighborhood. We've identified those routes as the 1, 43, SL4, and SL5 routes, due to their proximity near the Chester Square community.

Data Analysis

Summary of data, data types, and data preprocessing.

Expectation: Data sources match the problem statement and are appropriate. These descriptions should be very explicit so someone could read your page and properly reproduce your results.

Our data was mostly text files that we got from the MBTA GTFS data. We also manually created one csv file we needed. We used python to convert the text files we needed into CSV files, and then read those files using the d3.csv method. Most of our analysis came from mining the data through filtering techniques, where we got a better understanding of the particular use, and capabilities, of the packaged GTFS data.

What we initially discovered was how vast and well organized the GTFS data was, however we did realize that much of it was compartmentalized and we had to preform a number of joins in order to extract the exact data that we cared about. Namely, the stops around the chester square neighborhood and the bus routes which they were apart of. After extracting this data, and pulling their associated geographical coordinates, we were able to build out some helpful visualizations.

Task Analysis

Summary of task table.

The main objective of our visualization is to explain the unreliability of the MBTA bus system in an effective manner. We first gathered and parsed through data on the expected versus actual arrival times of the bus and discovered the percent of reliability of each bus line that travels through the Chester Square Neighborhood. This analysis shows the where the MBTA should work to improve the reliability and usage of the buses.

Our Initial Drawing

In our initial visualization plans we drew up nine different possibilities of visualizations, however we felt that this was the most comprehensive visual we came up with, as it included multiple visuals in one layout, but also linked the two in information, and could include brushing and linking

Our Initial Visualization (via Google Slides)

These are the first mock-ups we made in google slides using separate shapes to get a particular idea about how we would like it to be laid out. The idea was to have two visuals situated in a container and have them be reflective of one another. One visual would focus on the Chester Square neighborhood specifically, and the other would be on for the entire routes that the stops are apart of.

Our Second Visualization (via Google Slides)

The second visualization is a more in-depth version of the first showing the interaction possibilities of the visualization.

Our Third Visualization (via Google Slides)

In the third visualization we see a specific instance of interaction when the user selects a drop-down to help filter out the results and to show only the route one bus stops and the route one bus line. This filtering can be done for any of the bus-routes.

Our Fourth Visualization (via Google Slides)

In the fourth visualization we see a very similar interaction method but on the focus of the direction of the bus. We wanted to know if there was an impactful difference on the bus routes direction and its ability to be adherent to its schedule.

Our Fifth Visualization (via Google Slides)

Our fifth visualization shows power-user interaction which combines the first two drop-downs of interaction but in the form of interacting with the visual itself. By clicking on a particular bus route, or vice versa (and not seen in the visual) clicking on the route, then you'd get information from the visual that way.
